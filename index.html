Staple terms, plain English

Deposit
The amount of your own money you contribute towards buying the property. A larger deposit usually means you need to borrow less.
Refinance
Replacing your existing home loan with a new one, either with your current lender or a different lender, to access a better interest rate, different loan features, or your available equity.
Principal
The amount of money you borrow from the lender to buy a property. As you make repayments, your principal gradually reduces.
Interest
The cost of borrowing money from the lender. It is calculated as a percentage of your remaining loan balance.
Equity
The portion of the property's value that you own. It is the property's current market value minus the remaining balance of your home loan.
Broker
A mortgage broker compares home loans from a panel of lenders, recommends suitable options based on your circumstances, and helps manage the application process. In most cases, brokers are paid by the lender rather than the borrower.
LVR (Loan-to-Value Ratio)
The size of your loan compared to the property's value, expressed as a percentage. For example, borrowing $400,000 to buy a $500,000 property gives you an LVR of 80%.
LMI (Lenders Mortgage Insurance)
A one-off insurance premium usually required when borrowing more than 80% of a property's value. It protects the lender—not you—if you're unable to repay your loan.
Pre-Approval
A conditional letter from a lender stating how much they are likely willing to let you borrow. This gives you a clear budget when house hunting.
Comparison Rate
A rate that combines the interest rate with most loan fees and charges, giving you a better idea of the overall cost of a loan compared with others.
First Home Owner Grant (FHOG)
A government payment available to eligible first home buyers, usually when buying or building a new home. Eligibility and grant amounts vary between each state and territory.
Settlement
The day ownership of the property officially transfers to you, and your lender pays the purchase funds to the seller.
Guarantor
Someone, usually a parent or close family member, who offers part of their property's equity as security to help you qualify for a home loan or reduce the need for Lenders Mortgage Insurance.
Variable Rate
An interest rate that can rise or fall over time. Your repayments may change when the lender adjusts its rates, offering greater flexibility but less repayment certainty.
Fixed Rate
An interest rate that is locked in for a set period (usually 1 to 5 years). Your repayments stay the same during this time, providing greater budgeting certainty.
Interest Only
A loan where, for an agreed period (usually 1 to 5 years), your repayments cover only the interest charged rather than reducing the amount you borrowed. While repayments are lower during this period, you'll generally pay more interest over the life of the loan.
Construction Loan
A loan designed for building a home rather than buying one. The money is released in stages as the build progresses, and you typically only pay interest on the amount drawn down so far.
Split Loan
A home loan that combines both fixed and variable interest rates, allowing you to divide your loan between the two.
Offset Account
An everyday transaction account linked to your home loan. The balance in the account reduces the amount of your loan charged interest, helping you save on interest over time.
Redraw Facility
A feature that allows you to make additional repayments on your home loan and access those extra funds later if needed, subject to your lender's conditions.
